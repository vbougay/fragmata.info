Cyprus in late July is an island doing its best impression of a frying pan. The thermometer keeps flirting with numbers that should legally require a siesta, the sea feels like soup, and the only creatures moving with any enthusiasm between noon and five are cicadas and delivery scooters.

Our reservoirs feel it too. Water has two great enemies in a Cyprus summer — taps and the sky — and both are working overtime. Right now the 18 main dams hold **118 million cubic metres, 40.6% of capacity**, and the level is falling by about **149,000 cubic metres a day**. That's **1,727 litres every second**, around the clock, and from here the ledger only gets uglier: as the heat digs in, our own forecast model expects the drawdown to run even steeper through August. Faster and faster, until the first proper rains show up — which, on this island, is a date no one should bet a garden on.

### Remember rain? We kept the receipts

Here's the twist: this is the *good* year. The 2025/26 season delivered **115 million cubic metres of inflow** — six times the miserable 18.7 of the year before, the best season in four years. February through May carried most of it, nine dams filled to 100%, and Mavrokolympos went from literally-zero to two-thirds full. Last July the dams stood at 17.8%. Today it's 40.6% — **66 million cubic metres more water than a year ago**.

We spent that winter and spring doing what any serious monitoring project would do: standing on dam crests in the rain, filming water doing things water hadn't done here in years. Spillways roaring. Rivers arguing with their banks. Rain hammering reservoirs that had been cracked mud six months earlier.

Those clips were too good to leave in a photo album. So they became infrastructure.

### Introducing Zen Mode

**Zen Mode** is a full-screen, always-dark page with exactly one number on it: how much water is in the island's reservoirs *right now* — not as of the last bulletin, but **this second**, estimated live from the latest official reading, the trend of the past four weeks of bulletins, and the seasonal pattern from our 38-year forecast model. The number ticks. It does not stop ticking. As of this morning it reads about 118 million cubic metres and it is going down by roughly ten bathtubs a second.

Behind the number: the water itself. Thirteen vertical clips filmed at real Cyprus dams during this exceptional season, playing on an endless shuffled loop with smooth crossfades — **with sound on**. Flowing water, falling rain, the low roar of a spillway. It's the best ASMR this island has produced since the invention of the frappe shaker, and it doubles as a memorial to the wettest spring in four years.

A few notes for connoisseurs:

- Tap the number (or the toggle) to switch between **cubic metres** and **percent**. The m³ view is the dramatic one — the digits genuinely spin.
- The speaker button controls the water sounds. We default them on, because that's the entire point.
- The estimate re-anchors itself automatically every time a new WDD bulletin lands. Reload whenever you like — the number picks up exactly where physics left it.
- It runs forever. Put it on a spare screen, a TV, an office dashboard. It is the world's slowest thriller, and everyone on the island is in the cast.

### How much is 1,727 litres per second?

Numbers this size slide off the brain, so here is the current national drawdown in useful units:

- A **full bathtub every tenth of a second**
- **Ten water tankers every minute**
- An **Olympic swimming pool every 24 minutes**
- **149 million litres every day** — before you've watered a single geranium tomorrow

To be clear, this isn't "waste" — it's the island living its normal summer life: drinking water, showers, crops, plus the several thousand pools' worth the sun quietly steals off the reservoir surfaces. The dams are doing exactly what they were built to do: hold winter, and spend it through summer. Zen Mode just lets you watch the spending happen — honestly, hypnotically, one litre at a time.

### The serious part

Now put the frappe down for a second.

That +66 million cubic metres over last year? That's not a trend. That's **one lucky winter** — a single generous season sitting in an account that took three dry years to empty. The 38 years of records behind our forecasts say wet years on this island arrive in clumps, leave without notice, and are always followed by years that test how carefully we spent them. Nobody — not us, not the forecast model, not the optimists at the beach bar — knows what next winter will bring.

What we *do* know is the outflow side of the equation, because it's ticking on your screen: every second, 1,727 litres. Some of that second is yours.

So: fix the dripping tap — a slow drip is thousands of litres a year. Take the five-minute shower, not the fifteen. Water the garden at dusk, when the sun isn't drinking alongside the plants. Cover the pool. Sweep the terrace instead of hosing it. None of these will refill Kouris on their own — but 900,000 people making small boring choices is the only lever this island has ever actually controlled.

Open Zen Mode. Listen to what a good winter sounds like. Watch what a hot summer costs.

Then go close a tap. The number will notice — even if it's too polite to show it.
